# Advocate — Strategy Primer

The Advocate speaks for the Guilds — collect them, cycle them, and leverage them to dominate the Court across all three Acts. You're not defined by a single strong mechanic but by flexibility: your power scales with whatever Guild cards you've accumulated, so setup choices and Guild variety matter more than following a fixed build. Relics are your most important resource throughout; Psionics come second. The map and the Empire are secondary concerns, but neglect them at your peril — a rich Guild hand makes you the table's most tempting raid target.

---

## Act 1 — Seed the Court *(Complexity 2/5, Difficulty 2/5)*

**Objective focus:** Advance 18 times by triggering Prelude effects on Guild cards (which discard them) and using Guild Investigators to revive discarded Guilds. Aim for roughly 4 discarded Guilds (12 advances) and 3–5 Investigators uses (6 advances). You do not need to win ambitions — only place first or second in at least one declared ambition to satisfy Guild Overseers and keep using Guild actions.

- Prioritise Relic planets at setup; Psionics are a strong second. Combining Fuel + Psionic + Relic in a single turn lets you use Investigators, influence, and secure in one action.
- Don't discard Guilds in early chapters unless you can recover them — patience is rewarded. Use Investigators only when you can at least influence the returned Guild that same turn.
- End the Act holding Guilds in 3–4 different suits (excluding Guild Overseers); 2 suits or fewer means an uphill Act 2.
- Near the end of Chapter 3, run an "investigation party": flood the Court with revived Guilds so they reshuffle into the deck at Act end rather than getting scrapped.
- Secure gates and build ships proportional to your Guild count — you become an increasingly valuable raid target as your hand grows.

**Key kit:**
- **Guild Investigators** — your signature. Attaches any Guild from the Court discard to a Court card with no agents. Core advance engine and the tool that keeps Guilds in circulation for later Acts.
- **Guild Overseers** — a constraint, not a threat. As long as two ambitions are declared, qualifying off one of them is routine. Being tied for first counts; tied for second does not.
- **Silver Tongues** — trigger immediately: you advance, then it sits in the discard ready to be retrieved later. Don't try to hold it; its heat-to-value ratio makes early discard the right play.
- **Sworn Guardians / Keeper's Trust** — the only base-deck Guild without a self-discarding Prelude. Worth securing for raid protection and its suit value in later Acts.

**Notable matchups:**
- **Steward** — their Dealmakers can help you secure Guilds, especially if they've stacked agents on one. Cooperate selectively, but also consider becoming First Regent to make them fail: a failed Steward puts Dealmakers in the Act 2 deck, which is a huge prize for you.
- **Believer** — they want to attach Faith cards to Guilds; you want those Guilds. Influence a Guild, let them teach on it, then secure both. Every Guild you take without a Faith card attached is a blow to them.
- **Partisan** — they're your biggest long-term rival. If you can beat them on ambitions where they lack Outrage this Act and make them fail, you preserve your Guild hoard for Acts 2 and 3 in relative peace.

## Act 2 — Demand and Accumulate *(Complexity 1/5, Difficulty 1/5)*

**Objective focus:** Advance 12 times — 1 per chapter for each Guild suit you hold, plus 1 per chapter for each opponent who has fewer Guilds than you. With 3–4 suits entering Act 2, this nearly completes itself. Maintain 4 suits (not 5): holding all 5 blocks Advocate's Demand, your primary Favor engine. Acquire missing suits from the Court; use Demand to generate Favors and spend them on Guilds at Summits.

- Target protected Guilds with Advocate's Demand whenever possible — rivals are forced to pay a Favor rather than give them up, and Favors are generally worth more to you than the Guild you'd receive.
- Be First Regent when you can: Edicts trigger Advocate's Demand and the Imperial Demand generates Favors, which Guild Negotiations makes extremely powerful.
- Keep Guilds flowing at Act end: use Investigators to push cards back into the Court for reshuffling so Act 3 starts Guild-rich.
- Watch your agent supply — Diplomatic Fiasco can force you to discard Guilds beyond your agent count. Don't give too many Favors, and stay alert to Fiasco timing.

**Key kit:**
- **Advocate's Demand** — triggers every Edict. Demand a Guild suit you don't have: they give you the Guild (you pay 1 Favor) or they pay 1 Favor. Prefer the Favor; it compounds through Guild Negotiations and keeps your suit count at a productive 4.
- **Guild Negotiations** — Guilds are now tradeable. Makes every Favor substantially more valuable; 2 Favors can buy almost any Guild from a rival at a Call to Order.
- **Liaisons (added to deck after Act 1 success)** — fine as placeholder Guilds; more important for their suits than their effects. Psionic Liaison is the best: Influence appears on two suits and Empath is otherwise hard to contest.

**Notable matchups:**
- **Partisan** — they now compete for Guilds (one of their objectives rewards having many). Hit them often with Advocate's Demand, target their agent supply through Court actions, and try to make them fail: their Act 2 success adds an Edict that is very bad for you in Act 3.
- **Warden** — Fiefs let the Lord tax vassal cities, draining your Favor supply and setting up Diplomatic Fiasco. Try to be the Lord of any Fief you're in, or stay alert to your agent count.
- **Founder** — the Commonwealth offers protection; accept it. But if the Founder is set up to dominate Act 3 through Free cities, consider leaving to undermine them.

## Act 3 — Rule the Guild Economy *(Complexity 2/5, Difficulty 4/5)*

**Objective focus:** Two Grand Ambitions. The first (easy): hold Guilds in more suits than the current chapter number — maintain all 5 suits by chapter 4. The second (hard): more than half the cards in the Court must be Guilds at chapter end. For Act 3 court math: with no attached cards (5 total), you need 3 Guilds; with 5 attached cards (10 total), you need 6. Focus on the first ambition; pursue the second when conditions allow rather than forcing it.

- Guild Loyalty means your Guild cards survive Outrage — raid and ransack aggressively this Act without fear of losing your suite. Rivals cannot do the same to you.
- Use Guild Supremacy to lock down Tycoon, Keeper, and Empath: a few Guilds of the right suit makes rivals' resources almost irrelevant for scoring those ambitions.
- For the Court majority ambition: attach Guilds and Liaisons to Vox cards and the Council, target cards nobody wants to secure, and clear Vox cards with your own Secures to keep the deck cycling.
- Enter the final chapter with at least 2 Guilds per suit — rivals will try to strip your rarest suit to block the first Grand Ambition. Plan to re-acquire one if needed via Guildmaster, Demand, or a late raid.

**Key kit:**
- **Guild Loyalty** — Guild cards are now protected from Outrage. Transforms your risk calculus entirely; unlock aggressive raiding and ransacking you had to avoid before.
- **Guild Supremacy** — doubles Guilds' contribution to ambition scoring. Guildmasters (added to the deck on your Act 2 success) steal a Guild when you declare the matching ambition; these become the table's most contested cards.
- **Guildmasters** — directly steal rival Guilds while winning ambitions. Essential for topping up suits under threat and for the Guild-counting game against Partisan.

**Notable matchups:**
- **Partisan** — their first Grand Ambition is to hold the most Guild cards; yours requires all 5 suits. You will be in direct conflict all Act. Use Guild Investigators, Advocate's Demand, and Guild Loyalty as your advantages; stop their Grand Ambition or their easier second one (holding initiative) may carry them to victory.
- **Caretaker** — introduces four Unstable Golems and three Golem Worship cards to the deck (all Vox), directly sabotaging your Court majority ambition. Never hold an awake Golem longer than a single turn — their Act 3 Edict causes awake Golems to devour Guilds of their holder.
- **Gate Wraith** — Passage Storms is a persistent Vox card that recurs and occupies attachment slots, making the Court majority ambition harder. Their map contortions also ramp up aggression at the table, amplifying your raid vulnerability.
- **Planet Breaker** — breaking planets adds Guilds to the Court and forces rivals to discard Guilds, both useful to you. Let them operate freely in early chapters; intervene before they destroy a fourth or fifth building, as their Grand Ambitions are among the game's strongest.
